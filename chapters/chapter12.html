Chapter 12: Hands

The house was still. That kind of still where even the floorboards feel like they’re holding their breath.

He stayed on the floor longer than he needed to. I didn’t help him up. Mom was still between us. Her hand pressed to my chest, not forceful—just… there. A signal. A gate. A reminder that I could stop now.

I didn’t say anything. Neither did he. He stood eventually. Said nothing. Walked to the other room like it hadn’t happened. Like we’d both agreed not to tell on each other.

Sophie sat in the kitchen corner, her knees to her chest. Breathing shallow. Mom tried to talk to her. Soothe her. She flinched every time a voice got too loud. Even Abbey was quiet that night. When she came home, she didn’t joke. She lit a joint in the bathroom and said nothing.

I sat in the guest room. The bed creaked under me. The lamb was still there on the shelf. Still missing its eye. Still smiling. It made me feel sick.

It wasn’t comfort anymore. It was an accusation. A soft thing that had watched me become someone else. Someone my dad had made. Someone my mother couldn’t stop. Someone my sister feared.

I picked it up. Held it in both hands. It used to help me sleep. Used to remind me of my grandmother’s house. Safety. Warmth. A version of myself that hadn't learned to raise his voice.

Now? It felt wrong. Too light. Too soft. Too false. “You were supposed to protect me.” No answer. Of course not.

I ripped the lamb’s neck seam wider with my thumbs. Not all the way. Just enough to feel it give. Then I walked downstairs. Opened the trash. Dropped it in. Didn’t look back.

I went outside. Stood in the cold. Let the air bite. Mom came to the door, wrapped in her cardigan.

“Luke,” she said softly. “You okay?”

I didn’t answer. Just looked at my hands. They weren’t shaking. They weren’t clenched. They weren’t small anymore.
