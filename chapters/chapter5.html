Chapter 5: Polo Shirts

It started in junior high, when the boys around me stopped wearing uniforms and started wearing themselves.

Graphic T-shirts with band logos. Basketball jerseys. Weird designs with wolves and dragons and pop culture references I didn’t understand. One kid wore the same “Thrasher” hoodie every day until it smelled like a haunted skatepark.

I wanted in. Not to stand out. Just to blend. But in our house, T-shirts were “sloppy.” Unprofessional. Lazy. Dad said it like a curse.

“You’re not walking around like you’ve given up on life. Button up. Be proud.”

He made me wear polo shirts. Every morning. Stiff collars, heavy cotton, tucked in tight like I was going to a job interview for a position I didn’t apply for.

I hated them. I hated what they said about me before I ever opened my mouth. And it didn’t help that I was already different. Brown in a white school. Quiet when the other boys were loud. Too polite. Too observant. Too in-between.

The polos made it worse.

One day a kid muttered in homeroom, “Does he dress like that on purpose?” Another whispered, “He looks like he’s waiting for immigration paperwork.”

I laughed. Not because it was funny. Because I was already used to pretending things didn’t hurt. And it wasn’t like I could say anything at home. I tried once.

“I just want to wear a T-shirt. Like the other kids.”

“You want to look like a bum? You think that’s how people succeed?”

“It’s just a shirt.”

“No. It’s a signal.”

There was pride in the way he said it. But it sounded like a sentence.

Eventually, I stopped asking. Instead, I started sneaking shirts into my backpack. Folded tight. Hidden under my binders and gym clothes. I’d wait until I got to school, duck into the bathroom, and change before class.

For a while, it worked. Until it didn’t.

One morning, Dad walked into my room without knocking. He pulled open my bag and found a crumpled T-shirt wedged in the bottom. Said nothing.

The next day, he started checking my bag before school. Not always. Just enough to keep me guessing.

Sometimes he said things like:

“Anything in here I should know about?”

“Let’s make sure you’re not lying to your own father.”

“Be honest. It’s the only way people will respect you.”

I told Aiden and Chad about it at lunch. Laughed like it was a joke. “My old man’s got a collar fetish. Wants me to look like a bank teller.”

Chad squinted. “Bro. You’ve gotta be boiling in those.”

The next Monday, they showed up with shirts of their own.

Tossed them to me like a deal going down in a locker room. Aiden’s had a cracked Mario graphic on the front. Chad’s was just a black tee with a bleach stain that looked like a wolf.

“One for each day you grow a spine,” Chad said, grinning.

I didn’t say thank you.

I just smiled and slipped them into my locker, hidden behind my spare gym shoes and a half-empty deodorant stick. For a few weeks, I wore their shirts at school and changed back before I got home. But it didn’t last.

Dad caught me again. Didn’t yell. Just stared. Disappointed. Like I’d broken a covenant.

The next morning, there were no inspections. They just stopped.

I thought maybe he’d finally eased up.

But really, he’d just started noticing my sisters.

They were older now. Softer. More expressive. They cried more. And he didn’t like that. The attention shifted. And suddenly, I could breathe again. At least a little.

First day of high school, I wore a plain black tee. No logos. No slogans. No buttons.

Nobody said anything. But all day, I kept waiting to hear the sound of boots on tile.
