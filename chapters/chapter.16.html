Chapter 16: Legacy

My daughter flinched when I raised my voice.

Just once.

A small moment. Barely there. A thunderclap in my chest.

She was three.

Spilled orange juice on the floor. I was tired. Work had been relentless. Emails, deadlines, three client calls, and a Slack war with my dev team. I turned around too fast. Raised my voice without raising my heart.

“Come on, we’ve talked about this!”

That was all I said. And she flinched. Shoulders up. Hands near her face. Like she was ready for something worse.

I froze. The mug in my hand felt hot and stupid. She looked up at me. No tears. Just waiting.

I dropped to one knee. Said her name. Soft. Real. “Daddy’s sorry. That wasn’t okay.” She nodded. But she didn’t come closer. She just went to her mom and whispered something I didn’t hear.

Later that night, I couldn’t sleep. I sat in the living room and stared at my hands. These hands that had learned to code. To hold a steering wheel. To carry groceries. To throw a punch.

To build a life.

And maybe… To pass something down.

I used to say I’m nothing like him.

Now I wasn’t so sure.

Sometimes I get quiet around my kids. Not because I don’t love them. Because I’m afraid of what they might absorb from me.

My tone.

My pace.

My tension. The way my body tenses when a question feels like pressure. The way I “correct” too quickly. The way I demand silence just because the noise inside me is louder than the one outside.

At bedtime, my son asked me if monsters were real. I said no. Then paused. Then said: “Some monsters used to be good people. They just got stuck in their own hurt.” He looked confused. I kissed his forehead and turned off the light.

My wife asked if I was okay. I told her I was just thinking. She nodded. She knew what that meant by now.

The next day, I took the kids to the trail. Not for the club. Just us. I watched them walk ahead, laughing. Free. I walked slower. Behind them. Like a shadow that chose not to overtake the sun.

Legacy is a heavy word. People think it means wealth. Success. A name. But really? Legacy is tone of voice. It’s patience. Or lack of it. It’s the way you respond to spilled juice.

The lawyer emailed me that night. The case was moving forward. My father would be served in a matter of days. I stared at the screen for a long time. Then closed the laptop. And went upstairs. To tuck in my daughter. To be soft. To be safe. To start over.
