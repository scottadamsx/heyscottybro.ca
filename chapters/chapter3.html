Chapter 3: The First Time

It didn’t start with fists.

It started with something small. Something stupid. I don’t remember what. Maybe I forgot to do a chore. Maybe I left the milk out. Maybe I said “why” one too many times. What I do remember is how it felt. How fast it happened. How it went from tension to terror like a light switch snapping on.

We were in the kitchen. He said something I thought was unfair. I questioned it. He got upset. I pushed back. He didn’t like that. Voices rose. Mine was trembling, his wasn’t. Not yet. But it got there. Yelling. Spit on my face.

And then—he hit me.

A sharp crack across the side of my head. Not with a closed fist. Just enough to remind me who was in charge. Just enough to tip the balance. I stumbled. Not because it hurt—though it did—but because it felt like falling through the floor of the world.

I couldn’t breathe.

My heart exploded in my chest and then disappeared completely. My lungs forgot how to fill. My hands went cold.

A panic attack. That was my first one. I slid to the floor, knees to my chest, clutching my ribs like they might split open.

He stood over me like a monster in a dream. Breathing hard. Red-faced. Ashamed, maybe. But not of what he did. Of me. Of making him do it.

“You made me do that,” he said. “You pushed me there.”

Then he left.

The floor was cold. I remember the tile pressing into my shoulder. I don’t know how long I stayed there. At some point, I crawled upstairs.

The lamb was on my bed. I didn’t want to hold it. But I did. Not because it helped. But because it remembered who I used to be.
